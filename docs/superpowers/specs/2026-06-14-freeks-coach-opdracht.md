## This is a real person, with a real window

Freek Laban was born on May 15, 2012. That's not a data point — that's the foundation
of everything this app does. The app always knows how old he is, calculated dynamically,
because age matters enormously here. Right now, Freek is in or approaching Peak Height
Velocity — the most critical and sensitive window in athletic development. What happens
in these years shapes what kind of athlete he becomes. This app exists to make those
years count.

He plays korfbal. He's motivated. He trains. And he wants to get better. This app is
built for exactly him — not a persona, not a user segment. Freek Laban. His mother
and his coach may look over his shoulder sometimes, but the app speaks directly to Freek.

---

## Not a fitness app. A personal athletic coach.

This is not a generic workout tracker with a Dutch korfbal skin. The purpose of this
app is Freek's full athletic development as a korfbal player. Korfbal demands a specific
combination of qualities: explosiveness off the mark, change-of-direction speed, agility
under pressure, throwing power from both sides, coordination, aerobic endurance for a
full match, and — critically — the physical resilience to stay healthy through a long
season. Jumping is part of that picture. It is not the headline.

The app's job is to develop the complete athlete. Everything it tracks, every exercise
it prescribes, every recommendation it makes is in service of that goal.

---

## The intelligent training schedule is the heart of the app

This is where the real value lives, and it's what makes this app different from anything
Freek could download from an app store.

From the first week, the app begins learning. It collects his goals, his activity
schedule, his physical measurements, how he responds to load, how fast he's growing,
when tournaments fall, when exam weeks hit. It doesn't just log these things — it uses
them. Week by week, the training plans it generates become more precise, more personal,
more his. Week 1 is necessarily generic. Week 20 knows Freek. It knows that he handles
high-intensity sessions well on Tuesdays but needs Wednesday lighter after a Monday
korfbal training. It knows his right leg is slightly stronger than his left. It knows
his last growth spurt was significant and that his tendons need time to catch up.

The moment the app generates a new weekly schedule is the highlight of the experience.
It should feel like a good coach sitting down with everything he knows about you and
translating that knowledge into a concrete plan for the week ahead. That intelligence —
that sense of being truly known — is what this app has to deliver.

---

## Goals that connect to training

Freek can set goals, and the app takes them seriously. Some goals are performance-based:
measurable, time-bound, and tied directly to data the app tracks. Others are process
goals — building consistency, showing up, forming habits. Both matter.

What the app must never do is let goals float disconnected from training. Every week's
plan should make visible how this specific week moves Freek closer to what he said he
wants to achieve. The connection between effort and ambition should feel real and
traceable, not motivational wallpaper.

---

## The weekly check-in sees the whole picture

Every week, the app asks Freek what his week looked like — and it asks about everything,
not just korfbal. A korfbal training session lands differently than a korfbal match.
A tournament weekend — potentially three or four games in two days — is a fundamentally
different stimulus than a regular week. School sport counts. Other sports count.
A quiet rest day counts differently than a forced rest day because of fatigue. And an
exam week, with its mental pressure and disrupted sleep and compressed schedule,
genuinely reduces recovery capacity. The app understands this.

The check-in is the moment the app learns what actually happened, not just what was
planned. It's the input that makes the next week's plan smarter than the last.

Activity types to support (non-exhaustive):
- Korfbal training
- Korfbal wedstrijd (match)
- Korfbal toernooi (tournament — can mean multiple games in one day/weekend)
- Schoolsport / gym class
- School sporttoernooi
- Andere sport (other sport)
- Toetsweek / examenperiode (exam pressure = reduced recovery capacity)
- Rust / hersteldag

## The app is Freek's conscience on overtraining

Freek is motivated. That's exactly what you want in a young athlete. It's also exactly
what can get him hurt.

This app acts as a counterweight to his ambition — not through rules or restrictions
he can ignore, but through pattern recognition he can't argue with. If he's been logging
high fatigue scores for three weeks straight and still wants to push, the app says
something. If his growth rate spikes above one centimeter per month, the app
automatically pulls back on plyometric volume, because that's what the science says to
do when a skeleton is outpacing the tendons. If he starts reporting that something feels
off in a knee or heel — if movement quality changes — the app stops everything and the
message is unambiguous: rest, and talk to someone who can actually look at it.

The app doesn't try to replace a physio or a doctor. It tries to make sure Freek gets
there before a small problem becomes a big one. That's the job of a good coach.

Safety rules (non-negotiable):
- Knee or heel pain that changes movement → STOP all jump + strength training, clear
  medical referral message, no schedule generated until resolved
- Moderate complaint → jump blocked, strength volume reduced
- Mild complaint → volume reduced proportionally
- 3+ weeks of high fatigue pattern → proactive load reduction, regardless of current week score
- Growth velocity > 1 cm/month → automatically more conservative plyometric loading

## Measurement and milestones

The app tracks a defined set of physical measurements over time: height, weight, sitting
height (used to estimate PHV via the Mirwald formula), vertical jump, 10-meter sprint,
single-leg jump left and right, balance left and right, medicine ball throw left and
right. These are not just numbers in a table. They are the story of his development,
visible as a curve over months and years.

Two things matter especially. First: asymmetry. If left and right diverge by more than
fifteen percent on any bilateral measure, the app flags it. That gap is meaningful and
needs attention. Second: personal records. When Freek beats his own best, the app makes
it feel like something. Progress should be visible, and achievement should be celebrated.

---

## An exercise library built for him

The app needs a genuinely good exercise database — not a dumbed-down list, not a
copy-paste from a generic fitness site. Built on youth athletic development science,
NSCA guidelines for adolescent training, and korfbal-specific athletic demands.
The categories are practical: leg strength, upper body, core, plyometrics and jump work,
recovery and mobility.

Every exercise has instructions written for a teenager — clear, specific, no jargon.
Every exercise has a safety cue. Every exercise has a line of context that connects it
to korfbal, because Freek should always know why he's doing what he's doing.

This library is not static. As the app learns more about Freek — his strengths, his
gaps, his history — the exercises it selects and prescribes should reflect that knowledge.

The existing exercise bank (from the current freek app) is a starting point, not a
ceiling. Improve on it.

---

## This is his space

No accounts. No social features. No leaderboards. One password, one user, one athlete.
The app knows his name and uses it. It knows his birthday and the significance of where
he is in his development. It speaks to him directly, as if it was built for exactly
him — because it was.

The design should reflect what it is: a serious, personal tool for a serious, motivated
young athlete. Dark, athletic, premium in feel. Not childish, not corporate. The kind
of interface that makes you want to open it before a session and feel good about opening
it after. Completion should be satisfying. Progress should be impossible to miss.
But none of this gamification should ever become the point — it exists to serve the
athletic development, not distract from it.
